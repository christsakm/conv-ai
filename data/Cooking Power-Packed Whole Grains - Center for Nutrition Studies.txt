Cooking Power-Packed Whole Grains - Center for Nutrition Studies [<p>Whole grains are an important part of a healthy diet, providing the complex carbohydrates that our bodies prefer as their main source of energy, as well as fiber and an assortment of vitamins, minerals, and other micronutrients. Unlike processed grain products like white rice and white bread, <a href="https://nutritionstudies.org/whole-grains-good-or-bad/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">whole grains</a> actually help to control our weight by making us feel full longer. Yet, many people don’t know how to cook whole grains and are intimidated by the process.</p>, <p>Here’s the good news: There are some easy ways to add whole grains to your diet, and most are very simple to cook.</p>, <p>The following chart of cooking techniques is from the <a href="https://wholegrainscouncil.org/recipes/cooking-whole-grains" rel="noopener" target="_blank">Whole Grains Council</a>. Note: Rinse grains before cooking. The grains will roughly triple in volume once cooked.</p>, <p>For the stove top, simply put the dry grain in a stock pot or saucepan with water or vegetable broth amounts listed below. Bring it to a boil, then simmer until the liquid is absorbed.</p>, <p>Grains vary in cooking times. Some of the variables depend on the age of the grain, the variety, and the pan being used for cooking. If the grain is not as tender as you like when the cooking time is up, simply add more water and continue cooking.</p>, <p>One the other hand, if it is tender and everything looks good before the liquid is all absorbed, simply drain the excess and serve.</p>, <p>Most whole grains will cook perfectly in a slow cooker.
